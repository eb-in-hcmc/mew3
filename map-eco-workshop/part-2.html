Warm-up

Start with the back of a napkin

Before layering in complexity, begin simply. A rough sketch forces you to commit to what matters most — which relationships, which actors, which tensions. Complexity comes after, not before, that core clarity.

Activity
"Back of Napkin" Mapping
  1. Quickly sketch your workplace, project, or community as a rough map. Give yourself 3 minutes. No rules on what to draw.
  2. Circle the element that feels most central to the problem you are working on.
  3. Draw one line outward from it to something that helps it — and one line to something that creates friction.
  4. What question comes to mind? Is it shared with your group, a personal curiosity, or a representational challenge?
Speed round

Share your draft ecosystems in the chat. What is the central question your map raises?

Core framework

Multilevel analysis: the 4M framework

No matter where you are in your process, your analysis benefits from thinking across levels. The 4M framework describes four scales of activity — from top-down programmatic structures to granular observations that revise our understanding of how abstract processes actually work.

Each level involves a different cognitive goal and calls for a different visual approach. Use the table below to identify where your group currently sits — and which level you need to move toward next.

Development Stage Cognitive Goal Visual Tool Analytic Outcome
1Resource Mapping Offload — clear working memory of details. Nodes & Clusters: grouping related items. Identifying what exists vs. what is missing.
2Lines of Activity Simulation — replaying or imagining workflows. Vectors / Arrows: showing flow and dependency. Identifying efficiency (speed, friction, loops).
3Visualizing Value Evaluation — looking for evidence of benefits beyond intention. Line strength / Stars: indicating durable relationships and realized value. Identifying evidence of reciprocity.
4Scale Modification Synthesis — connecting macro to micro. Concentric Layers: showing hierarchy and tensions. Identifying causality (why the local friction exists).
Discussion

How do you know when you have shifted to a different level of analysis? What signals — in the map or in the conversation around it — tell you it is time to move up or down a scale?

Technique deep-dive

Circles in circles: multilevel time and relationship

Concentric rings represent hierarchy, time cycles, and relationship dependencies simultaneously. The outermost ring typically holds macro forces — policy, culture, sector conditions. Each inner ring gets more local, more immediate, more within reach.

Tensions appear at the borders between rings: the places where macro forces press down on local practice, or where local observations challenge our assumptions about how the larger system works.

If you want to use nested circles

What are the strengths or challenges in your group's chosen context? Which ring do your observations sit in — and which ring produces the friction you're trying to name?

Leveling up

Integrating data and AI: help or obstacle?

Data can sharpen your map — or it can bury the insight you are trying to surface. The key is knowing what decision the data is meant to support, and whether adding it brings more clarity than it costs in complexity.

Value through partnerships

Which relationships in your map are durable? Which are new? Data can help you distinguish established flows from emerging ones.

Value through collaboration

What outcomes exceed course or program objectives? Look for evidence of reciprocity that goes beyond the intended design.

Blocked value

Where are tensions or open contradictions preventing value from flowing? Data can make invisible friction visible.

What can you do? What can you do with others? What can you do with resources?

Would data help clarify your picture — or complicate it?

Where does AI assist your mapping process, and where does it hobble your own reasoning?

Closing

Group critique

After this session, use your smaller groups to test your map in discussion. The purpose is not polish — it is whether the map generates the conversation you intended.

Guiding questions

Does the map prompt the conversation you intended? What would you add, remove, or shift to make it more generative? Where does the map surprise someone who wasn't involved in making it?
