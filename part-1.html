Where we begin

Mapping as a cognitive instinct

Ecosystem mapping can look intimidating — layers, frameworks, arrows going everywhere. But it extends from something you do every day without thinking about it.

Your brain uses cognitive mapping to mark distance and time. Consider a simple errand: can you walk to the pharmacy in five minutes? You mentally retrace the route, estimate traffic, factor in other stops. You are already mapping — weighing relationships, distances, and decisions — entirely inside your head.

Ecosystem mapping asks you to do the same thing outward, with others, on a shared surface. The challenge is not the skill itself. It is merging your internal map with someone else's, building a shared visual vocabulary that holds multiple perspectives at once.

What you do naturally

Judge distance between ideas, people, and resources. Imagine paths between them. Anticipate friction or shortcuts. Make decisions about what is near and what is far.

What mapping adds

A shared surface. A visual vocabulary others can read. The ability to hold complexity still long enough for a group to think together — and further.

What a good map does for a group

Before diving into technique, it helps to understand what you are trying to achieve. Ecosystem maps serve several distinct functions — and knowing which one you need shapes every choice you make about how to build it.

1Reduces cognitive load by holding complex data in one shared frame.
2Facilitates discussion about priorities and missing elements.
3Clarifies issues and opportunities that are hard to see in text.
4Provides background quickly to educate new collaborators.
5Compresses data into visible consequences and relationships.
6Shows your reasoning transparently to others.
7Builds shared logic across different perspectives.
8Primes partners for meaningful, coordinated action.
Visual vocabulary

Do shapes have meaning?

We instinctively read stories into shapes. The Heider–Simmel study showed participants simple animated triangles and circles. Without any instruction, they described chases, intentions, and emotions. Our minds impose narrative on visual objects in motion.

This is both a challenge and an opportunity. If your collaborators will impose stories on the shapes in your ecosystem regardless, can you anticipate which stories? Can you design your map so the shapes guide people toward the conversation you want to have?
